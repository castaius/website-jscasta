**Version:** 1.0 working draft  
**Status:** Ready for implementation  
**Owner:** Juan Sebastián Castañeda / JSCASTA  
**Purpose:** This document defines how the JSCASTA business creates, delivers, and captures value. It is the primary strategic source of truth for the website, CRM, content, outreach, operations, and future team members.

## 1. Business Purpose

### Purpose

JSCASTA creates engaging experiences, ideas, content, and tools that help people experience and understand magic more deeply.

The business begins with performance. Through live magic, psychology, humour, storytelling, and audience interaction, JSCASTA creates moments that are entertaining, surprising, and socially engaging.

The work also explores magical thinking: the ways people use intuition, intention, perception, symbolism, belief, connection, and imagination to interpret their experiences and relate to the world.

Over time, the business will express this purpose through three connected areas:

1. **Live performance**  
   Engaging and entertaining in-person experiences that bring people together and create memorable shared moments.

2. **Magical thinking**  
   Ideas, frameworks, research, and public conversations that help people understand how magic, perception, belief, intuition, imagination, and meaning operate in human experience.

3. **Content, tools, and education**  
   Entertaining online content, educational resources, frameworks, and products that help people explore and understand their own relationship with magic.

For commercial clients, especially event organizers, the immediate value is practical. JSCASTA helps create energy, encourage interaction, make shared moments memorable, and give guests something meaningful to talk about.

For audiences and followers, the value extends into entertainment, curiosity, reflection, and learning.

### Long-term ambition

JSCASTA aims to become:

> A leading voice on magical thinking, one of the most engaging and entertaining performers for live shared moments, and a trusted creator of content and tools that help people understand and develop their relationship with magic.

### Strategic role of each area

- **Live performance** generates revenue, relationships, credibility, stories, footage, and real-world proof.
- **Content** expands reach, introduces people to the personality and ideas behind the brand, and builds an audience.
- **Magical thinking** gives the brand a distinctive intellectual territory and long-term authority.
- **Tools and education** turn ideas into practical resources and scalable products that people can use without booking a live performance.

These areas should reinforce one another rather than operate as disconnected projects.

### Current commercial focus

The long-term business is broad, but the current commercial focus is narrow:

> Corporate live performance is the primary entry point into the Sydney market.

This focus gives the business a clear offer, a defined buyer, a practical sales path, and a way to generate local proof and relationships.

Magical thinking, content, and educational tools remain part of the long-term system, but they should not compete with the immediate commercial objective on the first version of the website.

### Decision

> JSCASTA will grow as one connected ecosystem built around live performance, magical thinking, entertaining content, and educational tools.

### Operating implication

Every future project should support at least one of these areas and ideally strengthen more than one.

A project should be postponed when it does not contribute meaningfully to:

- current revenue,
- audience growth,
- authority in magical thinking,
- reusable intellectual property,
- stronger client relationships,
- or the long-term JSCASTA brand.

## 2. Customer Segments

JSCASTA serves different groups, but they should not all receive equal attention at the same time.

The business needs a clear distinction between:

- the customer who is most likely to pay now,
- secondary customers who may still be commercially valuable,
- the wider audience that supports long-term brand growth,
- and groups that are intentionally deprioritized during the current phase.

### 2.1 Primary paying customer now

The primary paying customer is a corporate event buyer in Sydney or the surrounding market.

This may include:

- event agency producers,
- corporate event planners,
- executive assistants,
- marketing managers,
- People and Culture managers,
- conference producers,
- hotel and venue event coordinators,
- and business owners organizing premium client or staff events.

These buyers are usually not looking for “magic” in the abstract.

They are trying to solve practical event problems such as:

- creating energy in the room,
- making networking feel less awkward,
- helping guests interact,
- filling quiet or transitional periods,
- providing entertainment that works without complex production,
- giving attendees something memorable to talk about,
- and making the organizer look competent and thoughtful.

The website, inquiry process, sales messaging, and outreach should be designed primarily for these buyers.

### 2.2 Primary use cases

The strongest current use cases are:

- corporate cocktail receptions,
- networking events,
- conferences,
- awards nights,
- client functions,
- company celebrations,
- product or brand events,
- and event-agency activations where roving entertainment is useful.

The current priority is interactive strolling magic because it is flexible, easy to integrate, and directly supports guest interaction.

### 2.3 Secondary paying customers

Secondary paying customers may include:

- private event hosts,
- wedding planners,
- hospitality venues,
- festivals,
- universities,
- schools,
- cultural organizations,
- brands commissioning content,
- organizations booking workshops,
- and clients seeking virtual or hybrid experiences.

These groups may still generate revenue, but they should not shape the first version of the website or the immediate sales system.

They can be served selectively when the opportunity is strong, strategically useful, or well aligned with the brand.

### 2.4 Wider audience and future customers

The wider audience includes people interested in:

- magic,
- psychology,
- perception,
- intuition,
- creativity,
- magical thinking,
- storytelling,
- performance,
- and personal meaning.

This audience may initially engage through:

- short-form content,
- long-form videos,
- interviews,
- articles,
- educational posts,
- performances,
- and downloadable resources.

Over time, some of these people may become customers for:

- digital products,
- educational tools,
- workshops,
- talks,
- books,
- courses,
- memberships,
- or live events.

The wider audience is important for long-term authority and scalability, but it should not dilute the current corporate sales message.

### 2.5 Geographic focus

The immediate geographic focus is Sydney and nearby markets in New South Wales.

This does not prevent work in other Australian cities or international markets.

However, local proof, local relationships, local search visibility, and repeatable access to Sydney-based corporate buyers are the current priorities.

### 2.6 Deliberately deprioritized groups

The business is not currently optimizing its website or outreach for:

- children’s birthday parties,
- low-budget private bookings,
- general public entertainment with no strategic value,
- tutorial seekers,
- hobby magicians looking only for free methods,
- or audiences that require a completely separate brand message from the current corporate focus.

This does not mean these opportunities can never be accepted.

It means they should not influence the central positioning, page structure, or main call to action during the current phase.

### Decision

> For the current growth phase, the primary paying customer is the Sydney corporate event buyer, especially event planners, agencies, executive assistants, marketing teams, and conference organizers.

### Operating implications

- The homepage should speak first to corporate event buyers.
- The main offer should be interactive strolling magic for professional events.
- The primary call to action should be designed for event inquiries.
- Proof should help a local buyer feel confident about professionalism, audience engagement, and reliability.
- Secondary services should remain available without competing with the primary message.
- Content for the wider audience should build authority without confusing the corporate sales journey.

## 3. Problems We Solve

Clients do not hire JSCASTA only because they want to see magic. They hire because they want an event to feel more engaging, connected, memorable, and professionally managed.

The value of the performance begins with understanding what is happening in the room before JSCASTA arrives.

### 3.1 Problems faced by the event organizer

The organizer may be responsible for an event that is expensive, visible, and difficult to control once guests arrive.

Common problems include:

- guests staying within familiar groups,
- networking that feels forced or uncomfortable,
- low energy during arrival, cocktail, or transition periods,
- empty moments before dinner, speeches, awards, or programmed entertainment,
- concern that the event may feel generic or forgettable,
- uncertainty about whether entertainment will suit a professional audience,
- fear that a performer may embarrass guests, interrupt conversations, or demand too much attention,
- limited time to manage another supplier,
- and pressure to justify the event budget to colleagues, leadership, or a client.

The organizer needs entertainment that contributes to the event without becoming another operational problem.

### 3.2 Problems faced by event agencies and planners

An agency or professional planner has an additional layer of responsibility: their own reputation is attached to every supplier they recommend.

They need performers who:

- communicate clearly and promptly,
- understand the event brief,
- arrive prepared and on time,
- adapt to changes in the room,
- interact appropriately with executives, clients, staff, and guests,
- require minimal technical support,
- coordinate well with venues and production teams,
- and make the agency or planner look reliable.

A talented performance is not enough if the booking process or event delivery creates stress.

### 3.3 Problems faced by the organization paying for the event

The organization may be investing in the event to:

- strengthen client relationships,
- reward or connect employees,
- support internal culture,
- launch a product,
- host industry partners,
- create brand goodwill,
- or make a conference more valuable.

Its risk is that the event becomes a pleasant but passive gathering that produces little meaningful interaction or lasting memory.

The organization needs the event to feel worth the investment.

### 3.4 Problems experienced by guests

Guests may arrive with very different levels of confidence, familiarity, and interest.

They may:

- know only a few people,
- feel uncomfortable introducing themselves,
- remain attached to colleagues they already know,
- wait passively for the next scheduled activity,
- become distracted or disengaged,
- or feel cautious about participating in entertainment.

The performance must lower social friction rather than increase it.

Guests should feel included, entertained, and free to engage without pressure or embarrassment.

### 3.5 The central event problem

The central problem can be summarized as follows:

> A room full of people does not automatically become an engaging shared experience.

An event can be well funded, well designed, and well attended while still feeling socially flat.

The business opportunity is to help transform passive attendance into active engagement.

### 3.6 The change JSCASTA creates

Through short, interactive moments performed for small groups, JSCASTA helps:

- create immediate points of conversation,
- bring unfamiliar guests into a shared experience,
- increase energy without interrupting the event,
- make transitional periods feel intentional,
- generate genuine reactions and memorable stories,
- and give the organizer visible evidence that guests are enjoying themselves.

The performance moves through the event rather than requiring the entire event to stop and reorganize around it.

### 3.7 Emotional and professional outcomes

The practical outcome is greater interaction and energy.

The emotional outcome is a room that feels warmer, more curious, and more alive.

The professional outcome is that the organizer, planner, or agency feels confident that the entertainment strengthened the event and reflected well on their decision.

### Decision

> JSCASTA’s primary commercial value is helping professional events become more socially engaging, energetic, memorable, and easy to manage through interactive live performance.

### Operating implications

- Marketing should describe recognizable event problems before describing techniques or credentials.
- Sales conversations should explore the event’s goals, room dynamics, schedule, audience, and possible low-energy moments.
- Proposals should connect the performance format to the client’s desired outcome.
- Testimonials should be requested around guest engagement, professionalism, adaptability, and the organizer’s experience.
- Case studies should show what changed in the room, not merely state that magic was performed.
- The website should reassure buyers that the performance is respectful, flexible, and operationally simple.

## 4. Value We Create

JSCASTA creates value at several levels at the same time.

The performance must work for the person booking it, the organization paying for it, the guests experiencing it, and the long-term business.

The strongest offers are those where all four levels reinforce one another.

### 4.1 Value for the event organizer

For the organizer, JSCASTA provides a practical and visible way to improve the atmosphere of the event.

The organizer receives:

- a performer who can work within the existing event structure,
- entertainment that does not require the room to stop,
- a flexible format that can adapt to timing changes,
- a clear way to create energy during arrivals, networking, cocktails, and transitions,
- a professional supplier who understands the event brief,
- and a memorable guest experience that reflects well on the booking decision.

The organizer should feel that the entertainment reduced pressure rather than adding to it.

### 4.2 Value for the agency or planner

For an agency or planner, the value includes both performance quality and supplier reliability.

JSCASTA should help them:

- deliver a stronger result for their client,
- reduce the risk attached to recommending entertainment,
- avoid unnecessary technical or production complications,
- respond to changes in timing, room layout, and audience flow,
- and strengthen their own reputation as someone who makes good creative decisions.

The agency is not only buying a performance. It is buying confidence in the delivery.

### 4.3 Value for the organization

For the organization paying for the event, JSCASTA helps convert event spending into a more meaningful audience experience.

The value may include:

- stronger interaction between employees, clients, partners, or guests,
- a more energetic atmosphere,
- positive emotional association with the event or brand,
- increased memorability,
- content-worthy reactions and moments,
- and a sense that the event offered something distinctive.

The performance should support the event’s purpose rather than feel like an unrelated add-on.

### 4.4 Value for guests

For guests, the value is immediate and experiential.

They receive:

- entertainment that happens close to them,
- a shared moment that makes conversation easier,
- participation without pressure or humiliation,
- surprise, humour, curiosity, and connection,
- and a story they can continue discussing after the performance.

The guest experience should feel personal even when the event is large.

### 4.5 Value created by the performance format

Interactive strolling magic is valuable because it is:

- mobile,
- adaptable,
- low in technical requirements,
- suitable for small groups,
- easy to integrate into existing schedules,
- capable of reaching different parts of the room,
- and effective during moments that would otherwise have low energy.

Its value comes from how well it fits the event, not only from the impossibility of the effects.

### 4.6 Distinctive value of JSCASTA

JSCASTA’s distinctive value comes from the combination of:

- more than 17 years of performance experience,
- psychology,
- humour,
- storytelling,
- close audience observation,
- respectful participation,
- international professional experience,
- and an ongoing body of work around magical thinking.

This combination should create an experience that feels intelligent, entertaining, human, and easy to engage with.

The intellectual side of the brand should add depth without making the event feel academic or overly serious.

### 4.7 Value for the long-term business

Each performance can create value beyond the immediate fee.

A successful engagement may also produce:

- local proof,
- testimonials,
- repeat bookings,
- referrals,
- agency relationships,
- venue relationships,
- corporate contacts,
- photographs and footage,
- stories for content,
- ideas for future material,
- and greater credibility in the Australian market.

This additional value should be developed intentionally rather than left to chance.

### 4.8 Core value proposition

The current commercial value proposition is:

> JSCASTA provides interactive strolling magic for corporate events, using psychology, humour, storytelling, and impossible moments to create energy, encourage conversation, and give guests a memorable shared experience while keeping the entertainment flexible and easy for the organizer to manage.

This is a strategic working statement.

It may later be shortened or adapted for the homepage, proposals, outreach, and spoken sales conversations.

### Decision

> JSCASTA will sell the outcome of a more engaging, connected, and memorable event, delivered through a professional and adaptable interactive performance.

### Operating implications

- Packages should be organized around event needs and outcomes, not only performance duration.
- Website copy should explain the benefit before listing credentials.
- Proposals should show how the selected format fits the event schedule and audience.
- Sales calls should identify the desired atmosphere and event outcome.
- Client communication should reinforce ease, preparation, and reliability.
- Post-event follow-up should deliberately request testimonials, referrals, footage, and future opportunities.
- Content from events should demonstrate audience engagement and shared reactions rather than only show isolated tricks.

## 5. Offers and Revenue Streams

The business will eventually earn revenue through several connected areas, but the current phase requires one clear commercial offer.

The offer structure should distinguish between:

- what JSCASTA is actively selling now,
- services that can be offered selectively,
- and future revenue streams that require more audience, proof, systems, or intellectual property before they become priorities.

### 5.1 Primary offer now

The primary offer is:

> Interactive strolling magic for corporate events in Sydney and surrounding markets.

JSCASTA moves through the event performing short, highly interactive experiences for small groups of guests.

The performance is designed to:

- create energy,
- encourage natural conversation,
- help guests connect,
- make transitions and cocktail periods more engaging,
- and give the event a memorable shared experience.

The offer is particularly suitable for:

- cocktail receptions,
- networking events,
- conferences,
- awards nights,
- client functions,
- company celebrations,
- product launches,
- and selected brand or agency events.

### 5.2 Core package structure

The first package system should remain simple.

The recommended starting structure is:

#### Essential Experience

A focused strolling-magic engagement for smaller events or a defined period in the event schedule.

Typical duration:

- approximately 60 minutes.

Best suited for:

- smaller guest counts,
- arrivals,
- cocktail periods,
- and short networking windows.

#### Signature Experience

A longer strolling-magic engagement that allows JSCASTA to reach more guests and work across a wider portion of the event.

Typical duration:

- approximately 90 minutes.

Best suited for:

- larger receptions,
- conferences,
- awards nights,
- and events where guest interaction is a major objective.

#### Extended or Custom Experience

A tailored engagement for events requiring:

- approximately two hours or more,
- multiple performance periods,
- a combination of strolling and featured performance,
- custom material,
- brand integration,
- travel,
- unusual schedules,
- or additional planning.

This option should be quoted individually.

Package names are provisional. The important decision is to keep the buying structure understandable and avoid presenting a large catalogue of loosely related services.

### 5.3 Pricing direction

The current internal pricing direction for Sydney corporate work is:

- **60 minutes:** approximately AUD $1,100–$1,250
- **90 minutes:** approximately AUD $1,300–$1,500
- **Two hours:** approximately AUD $1,500–$1,800
- **Custom, stage, branded, or complex engagements:** quoted individually

The internal minimum target is approximately AUD $1,000 for a standard local corporate booking.

These figures are working commercial guidance rather than permanent public prices.

They should be reviewed after:

- real Sydney inquiries,
- booking conversion data,
- agency feedback,
- event size and complexity,
- travel requirements,
- local proof,
- and demand.

Public pricing should not be added automatically to the website until the business decides whether visible starting prices improve lead quality and conversion.

### 5.4 What the client is buying

The client is buying more than a number of performance minutes.

The offer includes:

- consultation around the event format,
- professional pre-event communication,
- preparation for the audience and schedule,
- arrival and coordination with the organizer or venue,
- live interactive performance,
- adaptation to the room and timing,
- and a reliable professional experience from inquiry through completion.

Duration remains useful for packaging, but value should be communicated through event outcomes, ease, and fit.

### 5.5 Add-ons and higher-value options

Possible add-ons or custom elements may include:

- a short featured performance for the full room,
- an emcee or hosting component when appropriate,
- custom material related to the organization or event,
- brand or product integration,
- multiple performance blocks,
- additional travel,
- content creation or approved event footage,
- workshops,
- talks,
- or hybrid and virtual elements.

These should not clutter the main offer.

They should appear only when relevant to the buyer’s brief or as part of a custom proposal.

### 5.6 Secondary service revenue

Secondary services may continue to generate revenue selectively.

These include:

- private events,
- weddings,
- festivals,
- university performances,
- talks,
- workshops,
- virtual shows,
- hybrid experiences,
- branded content,
- creative consulting,
- and selected stage performances.

A secondary opportunity should be evaluated according to:

- fee,
- strategic value,
- relationship value,
- content or proof potential,
- fit with the brand,
- preparation required,
- and whether it distracts from the current corporate-market focus.

### 5.7 Future scalable revenue streams

Long-term scalable revenue may come from:

- digital guides and PDFs,
- educational tools,
- courses,
- workshops delivered to groups,
- paid talks or keynotes,
- books,
- memberships or communities,
- licensed frameworks,
- magic products,
- performance tutorials,
- and resources related to magical thinking.

These revenue streams support the long-term ambition, but most are not immediate priorities.

They should be developed when there is:

- a clearly defined audience,
- demonstrated demand,
- a repeatable framework,
- sufficient proof,
- and a practical distribution channel.

### 5.8 Revenue relationship between the business areas

The intended long-term relationship is:

```text
Live performance
    → immediate revenue, proof, relationships and stories

Content
    → attention, trust, audience growth and discoverability

Magical-thinking authority
    → differentiation, speaking opportunities and intellectual property

Tools and education
    → scalable revenue and deeper audience value
```

The business should avoid expecting every area to generate revenue immediately.

Each area should have a defined role in the system.

### 5.9 Brand architecture boundary

JSCASTA is the central personal and professional brand.

Live performance, speaking, content, and public work around magical thinking can operate under JSCASTA when they share the same personality, authority, and audience relationship.

Separate product names or sub-brands may be useful for:

- specific educational systems,
- publishing projects,
- magic products,
- communities,
- or projects with a distinct audience and identity.

A separate brand should only be created when it reduces confusion or improves commercial clarity. It should not be created merely because a project feels different or exciting.

### Decision

> The primary revenue offer for the current phase is interactive strolling magic for Sydney corporate events, supported by a simple duration-based package structure and custom quoting for more complex engagements.

### Operating implications

- The homepage should present one primary offer.
- The inquiry form should collect the information needed to recommend a suitable engagement.
- The CRM should record event details, expected value, package, lead source, and next action.
- Proposals should connect duration and format to the event’s needs.
- Secondary services should not compete with the corporate offer on the main sales journey.
- Future products should be validated before substantial production work begins.
- Pricing should be reviewed using real lead, booking, and profitability data.

## 6. Customer Journey

The customer journey defines how a person moves from first awareness of JSCASTA to becoming a booked client, receiving the performance, leaving public proof, and returning or referring future work.

The journey should feel personal, professional, and easy to navigate.

Automation should support the process without making the client feel that they are being handled by a machine.

### 6.1 Awareness and discovery

A prospective client may first encounter JSCASTA through:

- Google search,
- the website,
- Instagram,
- TikTok,
- YouTube,
- LinkedIn,
- a referral,
- an event agency,
- a venue,
- a previous performance,
- a festival,
- or direct outreach.

Each channel should lead the prospect toward one clear next step:

> Tell me about your event.

The website is the primary conversion point for prospects who need more information before making contact.

### 6.2 Website visit

Within the first few seconds, a corporate buyer should understand:

- what JSCASTA provides,
- who the service is for,
- what outcome it creates,
- where the service operates,
- and how to inquire.

The visitor should then be able to:

- see relevant performance proof,
- understand how strolling magic fits into an event,
- review credibility,
- answer common concerns,
- and submit an inquiry without unnecessary friction.

### 6.3 Inquiry form

The inquiry form should collect enough information to begin a useful personal response without becoming burdensome.

Recommended fields:

- name,
- email,
- phone,
- company or organization,
- role,
- event date,
- event location,
- event type,
- estimated guest count,
- preferred performance duration,
- event goals,
- budget range,
- referral source,
- and an open message field.

The form should clearly explain that submitting an inquiry does not reserve the date.

### 6.4 Immediate confirmation

After submission, the client should receive an immediate neutral confirmation that the inquiry was received.

The message should:

- confirm successful submission,
- explain that the details will be reviewed personally,
- provide an expected response window,
- and avoid pretending that the immediate message was personally written.

The immediate confirmation exists to reduce uncertainty.

### 6.5 Personal response

JSCASTA should personally respond within approximately 2–4 hours during normal working hours whenever practical.

The response should demonstrate that the inquiry was read by referring to relevant event details.

The response may:

- confirm availability,
- ask for missing information,
- recommend a performance format,
- provide an initial price range,
- suggest a short call,
- or explain that a formal proposal will follow.

The goal is to feel attentive and human without creating unnecessary delay.

### 6.6 Qualification

Before investing significant time, the inquiry should be evaluated against:

- date availability,
- location,
- event type,
- audience,
- guest count,
- expected outcome,
- budget,
- schedule,
- technical or customization requirements,
- and strategic fit.

Qualified inquiries move toward a direct quote, discovery call, or proposal.

Poor-fit inquiries should still receive a courteous response.

Where appropriate, the lead may be:

- redirected to another performer,
- offered a simpler option,
- declined,
- or retained in the CRM for a different future opportunity.

### 6.7 Discovery call

A discovery call is not mandatory for every inquiry.

It is used when a conversation will improve the recommendation, reduce uncertainty, or increase the likelihood of closing a valuable opportunity.

A call is especially useful when:

- the event has several moving parts,
- the buyer is unsure what format they need,
- the event is high value,
- an agency or committee is involved,
- customization is requested,
- the schedule or room flow is unclear,
- or the buyer needs help justifying the entertainment internally.

Simple and well-defined inquiries may receive a recommendation and quote directly.

A discovery call should normally last approximately 15–30 minutes.

The call should clarify:

- the purpose of the event,
- the desired atmosphere,
- the audience,
- the schedule,
- likely low-energy moments,
- room layout,
- decision process,
- budget,
- special requirements,
- and what a successful result would look like.

The call should feel collaborative, relaxed, and useful rather than scripted or aggressive.

### 6.8 Recommendation and proposal

After enough information has been collected, JSCASTA should recommend the most appropriate option.

The proposal should normally include:

- a short summary of the event,
- the client’s stated goals,
- the recommended performance format,
- duration,
- what is included,
- package or option choices where useful,
- pricing,
- any relevant add-ons,
- contract,
- deposit requirement,
- payment schedule,
- validity period,
- and a clear booking action.

The proposal should be sent the same day whenever practical, ideally within a few hours of the conversation.

The proposal should make the next step obvious and minimize the amount of additional administration required from the client.

### 6.9 Proposal follow-up

An unanswered proposal should not be abandoned after one contact.

The Version 1 follow-up sequence is:

1. **First follow-up:** approximately two business days after the proposal.
2. **Second follow-up:** approximately five to seven days after the proposal.
3. **Final active follow-up:** approximately ten to fourteen days after the proposal, adjusted when the event date is close.

Each follow-up should add value rather than repeat “just checking in.”

It may:

- clarify availability,
- answer a likely concern,
- offer a brief call,
- restate the recommended fit,
- or explain the booking deadline.

After the active sequence, the lead may remain in the CRM for long-term follow-up when strategically appropriate.

### 6.10 Booking confirmation

A date is confirmed only when:

1. the client accepts the proposal,
2. the contract is signed,
3. and the non-refundable 50% deposit is paid.

The deposit compensates for reserving the date and clearing the schedule for the client.

Until all required booking steps are complete, the date remains available to other inquiries.

### 6.11 Final payment

The remaining 50% balance is due before the performance begins.

The preferred operational standard is payment before the event date.

Same-day payment may be accepted when agreed in advance.

Exceptions may be made for approved corporate clients, agencies, or organizations with formal internal payment processes.

Any exception should be documented clearly before the event.

### 6.12 Booking confirmation and onboarding

After the contract and deposit are received, the client should receive a confirmation message that includes:

- confirmation that the date is secured,
- a summary of the selected service,
- what happens next,
- key preparation deadlines,
- contact information,
- and any immediate questions or documents required.

The booking should also be added to:

- the CRM,
- the calendar,
- the revenue forecast,
- and the event-preparation workflow.

### 6.13 Event preparation

The preparation process should collect and confirm:

- final schedule,
- venue,
- exact performance times,
- event contact,
- guest count,
- audience profile,
- dress code,
- parking and access,
- arrival instructions,
- room layout,
- other entertainment or programmed activities,
- special guests,
- sensitive topics,
- brand requirements,
- photography and filming permissions,
- and final payment status.

Recommended contact points include:

- a planning check-in when needed,
- a formal confirmation approximately one week before the event,
- and a personal message approximately one day before the event.

### 6.14 Event delivery

The client experience includes every interaction on the day, not only the performance.

JSCASTA should:

- arrive with sufficient time,
- check in with the designated contact,
- confirm the current schedule and room conditions,
- adapt to reasonable changes,
- coordinate professionally with venue and production staff,
- maintain appropriate appearance and conduct,
- avoid pressuring or embarrassing guests,
- and confirm with the organizer before leaving when practical.

The performance should feel integrated into the event rather than imposed upon it.

### 6.15 Post-event follow-up

The day after the event, JSCASTA should send a personal thank-you message.

The message should:

- thank the client,
- refer to a specific moment or detail when possible,
- ask for honest feedback,
- and express interest in working together again.

When the response is positive, the next steps may include:

- requesting a Google review,
- requesting permission to use the feedback as a testimonial,
- requesting photographs or footage,
- confirming permission for a case study,
- and asking whether there is anyone else who may benefit from the service.

The Google review and testimonial serve different purposes and should both be pursued when appropriate.

### 6.16 Repeat business and referrals

The relationship should continue after the event.

The CRM should record:

- event details,
- client preferences,
- important people,
- audience reactions,
- useful personal notes,
- planning cycle,
- likely annual event timing,
- referral potential,
- and the next follow-up date.

The default long-term follow-up rhythm is:

- **approximately six months after the event:** relationship check-in for agencies, venues, and high-potential clients,
- **approximately nine to ten months after an annual event:** reconnect before the next planning cycle,
- **approximately twelve months after the event:** anniversary follow-up when no better timing is known.

The timing should be adapted to the client’s real planning calendar.

A lead that did not book may also remain in the CRM for future follow-up when the relationship or opportunity remains relevant.

### 6.17 CRM role

A CRM should store the journey and ensure that no qualified inquiry, follow-up, payment, event detail, review request, or repeat opportunity is forgotten.

HoneyBook is the provisional CRM candidate because it may combine:

- inquiry capture,
- lead pipeline,
- proposals,
- contracts,
- invoices,
- payments,
- reminders,
- and client communication.

The final platform decision must be confirmed after checking:

- Australian availability,
- payment support,
- tax and invoice requirements,
- contract functionality,
- pricing,
- integrations,
- and fit with the preferred client experience.

### Decision

> JSCASTA will deliver a personal, professional customer journey supported by automation, with a personal inquiry response within 2–4 hours, selective discovery calls, same-day proposals where practical, a non-refundable 50% booking deposit, final payment before performance, deliberate review requests, and planned repeat-business follow-up.

### Operating implications

- The website form must connect to a reliable lead-capture system.
- Immediate confirmations should be automated and clearly identified as confirmations.
- Personal responses must reference the actual event details.
- The CRM must track the next action for every active lead.
- A date is not marked as booked until the contract and deposit are complete.
- Payment exceptions must be approved and documented.
- Review, testimonial, referral, and repeat-business requests are standard parts of the journey.
- The process should be refined using real lead, conversion, payment, and repeat-booking data.

## 7. Growth Engine

The immediate purpose of the growth engine is to generate qualified corporate opportunities in Sydney that can convert into paid bookings.

Long-term brand building remains important, but the current priority is:

> Generate revenue and relationships from buyers who can pay now.

The growth engine should therefore emphasize direct access to decision-makers, local proof, repeatable outreach, referrals, and conversion-focused content.

### 7.1 Current growth objective

The current growth objective is to create a reliable weekly flow of:

- qualified corporate prospects,
- conversations with event buyers,
- discovery calls where useful,
- proposals,
- paid bookings,
- and relationships that can generate repeat work.

The business should not wait for organic visibility to develop before selling.

### 7.2 Priority channels

The first growth phase will prioritize four channels.

#### 1. Direct outreach

Direct outreach is the fastest controllable way to create opportunities while the local network and search presence are still developing.

Priority prospect groups include:

- event agencies,
- corporate event planners,
- conference producers,
- executive assistants,
- People and Culture teams,
- marketing managers,
- hotels and venues,
- and event suppliers with access to corporate clients.

The objective is to begin relevant conversations rather than send high-volume generic messages.

#### 2. Referrals and relationships

Referrals should be actively developed from:

- previous clients,
- performers,
- event professionals,
- venues,
- photographers,
- videographers,
- festival contacts,
- and anyone who regularly encounters event buyers.

Some relationships may be more valuable than a single booking because they can produce repeated access to clients.

#### 3. Local proof and search

The business needs Australian and Sydney-based proof.

This includes:

- Google reviews,
- local testimonials,
- event photography,
- audience reaction footage,
- venue and event references,
- and a website clearly positioned for Sydney corporate events.

Local search should be developed, but it should not be treated as the only immediate source of leads.

#### 4. LinkedIn and email

LinkedIn and email will be used together.

LinkedIn will help:

- identify relevant buyers,
- understand their roles and organizations,
- create familiarity,
- show professional credibility,
- and maintain visible relationships.

Email will help:

- send direct, personal outreach,
- share a concise introduction,
- link to the website or proof,
- and follow up in a professional format.

The business does not need to become a LinkedIn content expert before beginning outreach.

### 7.3 Weekly outreach system

The first outreach system should be simple enough to maintain alongside college.

A realistic Version 1 target is:

- research 20–30 suitable prospects per week,
- send 10–15 personalized first contacts,
- send scheduled follow-ups,
- record every contact in the CRM,
- and review results once per week.

The outreach process should be:

1. Identify a relevant person or organization.
2. Record why they are a fit.
3. Find the best contact channel.
4. Send a brief personalized message.
5. Offer a clear reason to continue the conversation.
6. Follow up at planned intervals.
7. Record the result and next action.

### 7.4 Outreach positioning

Outreach should lead with the buyer’s event needs.

The message should communicate that JSCASTA can help with:

- networking energy,
- guest interaction,
- cocktail and arrival periods,
- memorable shared moments,
- and flexible entertainment that is easy to integrate.

The first message should not attempt to explain the entire career or every available service.

Its purpose is to earn a reply, introduction, or short conversation.

### 7.5 Initial outreach audiences

The first prospect lists should focus on:

#### Event agencies

Why they matter:

- one relationship may produce multiple events,
- they regularly need trusted suppliers,
- and they value reliability, responsiveness, and low-friction delivery.

#### Corporate event planners and executive assistants

Why they matter:

- they often organize internal, client, and leadership events,
- they may need support making networking and social periods more engaging,
- and they can become repeat buyers.

#### Conference and venue professionals

Why they matter:

- they see recurring event demand,
- they may influence supplier recommendations,
- and they can provide access to many different organizers.

### 7.6 Follow-up for outreach

The Version 1 outreach follow-up sequence is:

1. First personalized contact.
2. Follow-up approximately three to four business days later.
3. Second follow-up approximately seven to ten days after the first.
4. Final light follow-up approximately two to three weeks after the first contact.

The tone should remain useful and respectful.

A prospect who does not respond should remain available for future contact when there is a relevant reason, new proof, seasonal timing, or an appropriate event opportunity.

### 7.7 Strategic lower-fee opportunities

A lower-fee event should ideally provide at least two of the following:

## 8. Operating Model

The operating model defines how JSCASTA is run week to week while balancing college, business development, content creation, client work, and long-term brand building.

The purpose is to create enough structure to produce consistent progress without turning the business into an inflexible schedule.

### 8.1 Current working capacity

JSCASTA can currently dedicate approximately part-time to full-time working capacity to the business because there is no separate job competing for those hours.

College classes run primarily from 6:00 p.m. to 9:00 p.m., with assessment periods that may create substantial temporary workload.

The business should therefore operate with two modes:

#### Standard operating mode

Used during normal academic weeks.

Target business capacity:

- approximately 25–35 focused hours per week.

#### Reduced operating mode

Used during major assessment periods, travel, illness, or unusually heavy performance weeks.

Minimum business responsibilities:

- respond to active inquiries,
- complete proposal follow-ups,
- protect booked events,
- update the CRM,
- complete essential client preparation,
- and maintain a small amount of high-quality outreach.

The purpose of reduced mode is continuity, not growth at full speed.

### 8.2 Current roles

At the current stage, Juan performs several roles:

- founder and strategic lead,
- performer and creative director,
- sales lead,
- marketing and content lead,
- and business operator.

These roles should be documented separately over time so that operational work can gradually be delegated.

### 8.3 Responsibilities that should remain with Juan

The following responsibilities are currently founder-dependent:

- live performance,
- final creative direction,
- final sales conversations,
- key client relationships,
- major pricing decisions,
- public communication of magical-thinking ideas,
- and final approval of brand-facing content.

These may be supported by systems or assistants, but they should not be fully delegated during the current phase.

### 8.4 Responsibilities that can later be delegated

Potentially delegable responsibilities include:

- prospect research,
- contact-list preparation,
- CRM data entry,
- follow-up reminders,
- scheduling,
- proposal assembly from approved templates,
- invoice and payment tracking,
- review-request reminders,
- file organization,
- content clipping and repurposing,
- basic website updates,
- research support,
- and weekly reporting.

Delegation should begin with repetitive tasks that have clear instructions and low strategic risk.

### 8.5 Role of Juan’s sister

Juan’s sister may eventually support the business even without advanced technical skills.

Suitable early responsibilities may include:

- researching event agencies and venues,
- finding contact names and public email addresses,
- updating simple CRM fields,
- checking whether follow-ups are due,
- organizing testimonials and client notes,
- maintaining prospect lists,
- collecting links, screenshots, and references,
- and helping track whether standard steps were completed.

She should not be expected to manage complex systems before the process is documented.

Her first role should be operational support using checklists, templates, and clear examples.

### 8.6 Active-project limit

JSCASTA will maintain no more than three major business-building projects at the same time.

The current active projects are:

1. Corporate website and inquiry experience.
2. CRM and customer-journey implementation.
3. Direct outreach and local client acquisition.

Content is treated as an ongoing business function rather than a separate temporary project.

A new major project may only begin when:

- one active project is completed,
- one is deliberately paused,
- or the new project clearly replaces a lower-priority activity.

Projects such as major digital products, Patreon, extensive tutorials, large SEO expansion, and new sub-brands remain parked unless formally promoted into the active list.

### 8.7 Weekly priority order

The default priority order is:

1. Active client needs and booked events.
2. New inquiries and proposal follow-ups.
3. Direct outreach and sales conversations.
4. College deadlines and class requirements.
5. Website and CRM implementation.
6. Content production and publishing.
7. Performance development.
8. Long-term magical-thinking research and product development.
9. Optional experiments.

College deadlines may temporarily move above business-building work when required, but active clients and booked commitments remain protected.

### 8.8 Weekly work structure

A standard week should normally include:

- daily checks for active leads and client needs,
- one prospect-research block,
- two outreach and follow-up blocks,
- one or two website and systems blocks,
- at least one protected content block,
- at least one performance-development block,
- one administration and finance block,
- and one weekly review block.

The structure may be completed through long focused blocks or shorter sessions depending on the task and academic workload.

### 8.9 Content as a core function

Content remains strategically important even while immediate bookings are the primary objective.

Content supports:

- visibility,
- trust,
- proof,
- audience growth,
- magical-thinking authority,
- future products,
- and long-term demand.

However, content should not consume unlimited time while the business lacks a reliable client pipeline.

The first content priority is material that serves multiple purposes.

Examples include:

- a performance clip that can support outreach, the website, LinkedIn, Instagram, and proposals,
- an event insight that demonstrates expertise to buyers while remaining entertaining to a wider audience,
- or a magical-thinking idea that can become a short video, longer article, talk segment, and future educational resource.

The operating principle is:

> Prefer content that creates several useful assets from one idea, event, or recording session.

### 8.10 Recommended time allocation

During standard operating mode, the working target is:

- **30% sales and outreach**
- **20% website, CRM, and systems**
- **20% content**
- **15% client preparation and performance development**
- **10% administration and finance**
- **5% long-term research and experiments**

These percentages are directional rather than rigid.

When bookings increase, client delivery and preparation will require more time.

When assessments become heavy, reduced operating mode applies.

### 8.11 Rest and leisure

At least one day per week should be protected from planned business work whenever active events and academic deadlines allow.

When a full rest day is not possible, the week should still include deliberate leisure periods without guilt or business multitasking.

Urgent client responsibilities may still require brief attention, but routine work should not automatically consume every available day.

### 8.12 Decision filter for new work

Before accepting a new project, activity, collaboration, or opportunity, evaluate:

1. Can it generate meaningful revenue soon?
2. Can it create access to valuable clients or relationships?
3. Does it build Sydney or Australian proof?
4. Does it strengthen the primary corporate offer?
5. Does it create reusable content or intellectual property?
6. Does it support authority in magical thinking?
7. What preparation and ongoing maintenance will it require?
8. Which current priority will it delay or replace?
9. Can it be completed well with the available time?

An exciting idea does not become an active project automatically.

### 8.13 Review rhythm

#### Weekly review

Review:

- active leads,
- proposal follow-ups,
- outreach activity,
- conversations,
- proposals,
- bookings,
- booked revenue,
- client work,
- content,
- website and CRM progress,
- college deadlines,
- and the next week’s top three priorities.

#### Monthly review

Review:

- revenue booked and collected,
- expenses,
- lead sources,
- conversion,
- average booking value,
- reviews and testimonials,
- local proof,
- content performance,
- time use,
- and tasks that should be stopped, delegated, or improved.

#### Quarterly review

Review:

- commercial focus,
- target market,
- offer and pricing,
- active-project list,
- website effectiveness,
- CRM effectiveness,
- delegation opportunities,
- content direction,
- and progress toward magical-thinking authority and scalable products.

### Decision

> JSCASTA will operate through a flexible 25–35 hour standard business week, a reduced mode for demanding academic periods, a limit of three active business-building projects, protected content work, deliberate rest, and recurring weekly, monthly, and quarterly reviews.

### Operating implications

- The calendar should contain recurring business blocks.
- Active leads and booked clients receive priority over speculative projects.
- Content remains protected but must support current or long-term business goals.
- No more than three major business-building projects may be active simultaneously.
- Juan’s sister should begin only with clearly documented, low-technical operational tasks.
- Repetitive work should be documented for future delegation.
- Rest should be scheduled rather than treated as leftover time.
- Time allocation should change as lead volume, bookings, and college demands change.
