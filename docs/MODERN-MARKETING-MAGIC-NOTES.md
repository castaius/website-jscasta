# Modern Marketing Magic - Working Notes

Source: `Modern Marketing Magic.pdf` by Magical Katrina, written June 2025.

These notes are a project reference, not a replacement for the book.

## Core Thesis

Katrina describes her growth from roughly $50k/year to over $250k/year as the result of repeatable business systems, not just better performance skill. The big pattern is leverage: automate repetitive work, delegate support tasks, build a consistent lead machine, become strong at sales calls, and keep improving the client experience.

For career progress, the lesson is blunt but useful: talent matters, but systems compound.

## The Five Big Levers

1. Automation
   - Use tools like Zapier, CRM workflows, and email automation to remove repetitive tasks.
   - Automate lead capture, review responses, calendar updates, first-touch emails, follow-ups, and reminders.
   - The standard is: if a simple task repeats more than a handful of times, build a system for it.

2. CRM
   - A CRM is treated as essential infrastructure, not optional admin software.
   - Track every lead, source, tag, client note, contract, payment, proposal, and follow-up.
   - Use staged automation: inquiry, discovery call, proposal, contract/payment, pre-event checklist, day-before confirmation, post-event review request, one-year follow-up.
   - Data creates business insight. Tags and reports reveal which event types, seasons, offers, and niches are actually producing revenue.

3. Team
   - Shift identity from solo performer to CEO of a business.
   - Delegate work that does not require the performer personally: admin, posting, research, SEO setup, editing, asset prep, website updates, and routine communication.
   - Use VAs for trainable general work and experts for work that directly affects brand quality.
   - Buy back time when the cost of help is meaningfully lower than the value of the owner's time.
   - Use task management tools so delegation does not become another thing to mentally juggle.

4. Sales Calls
   - Katrina treats sales calls as a core skill and a major income driver.
   - Her numbers show that a lower close rate can still be very profitable when lead volume and pricing are high.
   - Calls should feel warm, easy, and human. The client should feel understood and relieved.
   - She frames herself as a problem solver who happens to use magic, not simply a magician selling a show.
   - She uses confidence, humor, price anchoring, tiered packages, quick follow-up proposals, and fast response time.
   - Speed matters: she tries to call quickly after an inquiry, especially during high-volume days.

5. SEO
   - SEO is treated as a long-term machine that compounds over years.
   - The work is consistent, repetitive, and slow to show results, but can create major organic lead volume.
   - Her workflow uses keyword research, AI-assisted draft generation, VA execution, internal links, external links, image alt text, and scheduled publishing.
   - She recommends committing to a two-year content habit and starting with local, buyer-intent keywords.
   - Quality and ethics still matter: check for plagiarism, cite sources, avoid false claims, and personally edit high-stakes pages like bios and sales pages.

## AI Approach

Katrina uses AI heavily but skeptically. She sees it as useful for brainstorming, rough drafts, SEO drafts, difficult message rewrites, driving-time ideation, data pattern analysis, and proof-of-concept visuals. She warns that AI can confidently invent facts, so factual claims need checking.

Her strongest AI lesson is not "let AI replace your judgment." It is "use AI as a fast intern, then apply your experience, taste, and oversight."

## Website Implications

The website should be built as part of a business system, not just as a brochure.

Priority ideas:

- Clear lead capture connected to a CRM.
- Fast inquiry response path with event-type tagging.
- Strong proof: client logos, testimonials, press, video, recognizable event types.
- Package or offer structure that makes buying easier.
- Pages for specific search intents, locations, event types, and buyer problems.
- Content plan that can compound over months and years.
- High-touch assets for clients: FAQs, tech/rider docs, expectations, booking flow.
- Human voice and personality, because Katrina repeatedly stresses trust, warmth, and client relief.

## Career Lessons To Apply

- Do not wait to feel "ready" before building business systems.
- Track reality. Revenue, lead source, close rate, event type, seasonality, and client notes all matter.
- Raise rates with evidence and confidence, then learn from the response.
- Call leads quickly. Email-only selling leaves money behind.
- Build assets once and reuse them: templates, videos, riders, checklists, proposals, blog workflows.
- Hire slowly but start experimenting with delegation early.
- Spend time on work only you can do: craft, sales, relationships, positioning, and decisions.
- Join or build a peer group where people openly share numbers, systems, failures, and experiments.
- Treat SEO and reputation as compounding assets.
- Make the client feel safe, served, and proud to hire you.

## Short Version

Katrina's book is a systems playbook. The income growth comes from becoming easier to find, easier to hire, easier to trust, and easier to remember, while removing low-value work from her own plate.

For us, the website should support that same direction: strong positioning, trust signals, lead capture, repeatable follow-up, and search content that compounds.
